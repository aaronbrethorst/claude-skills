# Copyediting Rules

## Strunk & White — The Elements of Style

1. **Choose a suitable design and stick to it.** Decide on a structure before writing. Don't shift approach mid-piece.
2. **Make the paragraph the unit of composition.** Each paragraph develops one topic. New topic, new paragraph.
3. **Use the active voice.** "The dog bit the man" not "The man was bitten by the dog."
4. **Put statements in positive form.** Say what something *is*, not what it *isn't*. "He forgot" not "He did not remember."
5. **Use definite, specific, concrete language.** "It rained every day for a week" not "A period of unfavorable weather set in."
6. **Omit needless words.** "Because" not "owing to the fact that." Every word should tell.
7. **Avoid a succession of loose sentences.** Don't string together clause after clause with "and," "but," "so." Vary sentence structure.
8. **Express coordinate ideas in similar form.** Parallel ideas deserve parallel construction.
9. **Keep related words together.** Don't separate subject from verb, or verb from object, with long intervening material.
10. **In summaries, keep to one tense.** Pick past or present and stay there.
11. **Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.** The stress position is the end. Put the important thing there.

## Orwell — "Politics and the English Language"

### The Six Questions (ask of every sentence)

1. What am I trying to say?
2. What words will express it?
3. What image or idiom will make it clearer?
4. Is this image fresh enough to have an effect?
5. Could I put it more shortly?
6. Have I said anything that is avoidably ugly?

### The Six Rules

1. **Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.** Dead metaphors ("level playing field," "at the end of the day") are noise.
2. **Never use a long word where a short one will do.** "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Show" not "demonstrate."
3. **If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.** If the sentence reads fine without it, delete it.
4. **Never use the passive where you can use the active.**
5. **Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.** Write for the reader, not to impress.
6. **Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.** Rules serve clarity. When a rule makes the writing worse, ignore it.
